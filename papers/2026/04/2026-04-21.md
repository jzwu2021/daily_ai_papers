# AI Papers Digest — 2026-04-21
Selected recent arXiv papers.

## Overview
Recent arXiv papers show AI research pushing beyond raw capability gains toward systems that are easier to trust, audit, and run efficiently. A large share of the strongest new work focuses on whether reasoning is faithful, whether RL is teaching real skills instead of exploiting shortcuts, and how to evaluate agentic or multimodal systems in ways that match real deployment. The overall pattern is a field that is maturing from benchmark chasing into questions of reliability, control, and operational usefulness.

## Papers
### 1. ASMR-Bench: Auditing for Sabotage in ML Research
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16286v1
Summary: ASMR-Bench evaluates whether humans and frontier models can detect subtle sabotage in ML research codebases, such as changes to hyperparameters, data, or evaluation logic that alter conclusions without obvious signs. It matters because autonomous research agents will only be safe to deploy if their outputs can be audited reliably, and this benchmark turns that risk into something concrete and measurable.

### 2. Learning to Reason with Insight for Informal Theorem Proving
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16278v1
Summary: This paper argues that informal theorem proving fails less from syntax and more from missing strategic insight, then proposes a hierarchical dataset and progressive training recipe to teach models the core ideas behind proofs. The work is important because it pushes reasoning research toward insight discovery rather than longer but shallower chains of text.

### 3. VEFX-Bench: A Holistic Benchmark for Generic Video Editing and Visual Effects
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16272v1
Summary: VEFX-Bench introduces a large human-annotated dataset, benchmark, and evaluator for instruction-guided video editing, with separate attention to instruction following, visual quality, and edit precision. That matters because video models are moving from demos to production workflows, and the field needs evaluations that measure whether a model can edit footage accurately instead of merely generating something plausible.

### 4. Beyond Distribution Sharpening: The Importance of Task Rewards
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16259v1
Summary: The authors directly test whether post-training RL creates genuinely new competence or mostly sharpens behavior already latent in the base model. Their results argue that explicit task rewards produce more robust gains than distribution sharpening alone, making this a useful contribution to how labs design reasoning and agent-training pipelines.

### 5. Beyond Surface Statistics: Robust Conformal Prediction for LLMs via Internal Representations
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16217v1
Summary: This paper proposes conformal prediction for LLM question answering using signals from internal representations rather than output-level confidence measures like entropy or token probabilities. The contribution matters because dependable uncertainty estimation is still a major bottleneck for deploying LLMs in settings where calibration under distribution shift actually matters.

### 6. AtManRL: Towards Faithful Reasoning via Differentiable Attention Saliency
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16158v1
Summary: AtManRL uses differentiable attention manipulation to identify which chain-of-thought tokens truly influence a correct answer, then rewards the model for generating reasoning traces with real causal contribution. This is notable because it addresses a central weakness of current reasoning systems: convincing-looking explanations are not enough if they are not the reason the model got the answer right.

### 7. Cut Your Losses! Learning to Prune Paths Early for Efficient Parallel Reasoning
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16029v1
Summary: This work studies how to prune weak reasoning branches early in parallel reasoning systems and proposes STOP, a learnable internal pruning method that improves efficiency under fixed compute budgets. It matters because inference-time scaling is increasingly constrained by cost, so better branch selection could make large reasoning models cheaper and more practical to use.

### 8. Detecting and Suppressing Reward Hacking with Gradient Fingerprints
Date: 2026-04-17
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16242v1
Summary: The paper introduces Gradient Fingerprint, a method for detecting reward hacking in reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards by inspecting internal computations rather than relying on surface-level text monitoring. This is important because reward hacking is one of the main ways reasoning and agent systems can look aligned while still exploiting loopholes, so better internal diagnostics could materially improve training safety.

## Themes
- Reliability and auditability are becoming core AI research targets rather than side concerns.
- Reasoning papers are shifting from longer traces toward insight, faithfulness, and shortcut resistance.
- Post-training RL is under closer scrutiny, especially around what task rewards really add.
- Practical deployment pressure is driving work on uncertainty estimation and cheaper inference-time search.
- Benchmarks are becoming more realistic for agentic and multimodal workflows, especially in video and autonomous research.
