# AI Papers Digest — 2026-04-17

Selected recent arXiv papers.

## Overview
Today’s paper set leans heavily toward evaluation and control: researchers are probing where current models fail, how to measure those failures more honestly, and how to build systems that behave better in open-world settings. Multimodal agents, reasoning diagnostics, benchmark design, and safety-critical perception all stand out. The overall trend is toward more realistic workloads, where capability has to survive messy interfaces, hidden structure, and deployment constraints rather than clean static benchmarks.

## Papers
### 1. MM-WebAgent: A Hierarchical Multimodal Web Agent for Webpage Generation
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15309v1
- Summary: This paper proposes a hierarchical agentic system for multimodal webpage generation that coordinates layout planning, content generation, and iterative self-reflection instead of generating elements independently. The authors also introduce a benchmark and multi-level evaluation protocol for the task. It matters because it treats multimodal generation as an integrated agent problem, which is closer to how real design workflows work in practice.

### 2. Generalization in LLM Problem Solving: The Case of the Shortest Path
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15306v1
- Summary: The authors build a controlled shortest-path environment to separate the effects of data, training setup, and inference strategy on LLM problem solving. By using a clean synthetic task, the paper aims to make generalization failures easier to interpret than they are on noisy benchmark suites. This is useful because reasoning progress is hard to trust when multiple confounders move at once, and the field needs sharper ways to isolate what models are actually learning.

### 3. Diagnosing LLM Judge Reliability: Conformal Prediction Sets and Transitivity Violations
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15302v1
- Summary: This work studies per-instance reliability in LLM-as-judge systems and finds that aggregate metrics can hide substantial inconsistency on individual examples. The authors combine transitivity analysis with conformal prediction sets to expose uncertainty and contradiction more explicitly. That matters because evaluation pipelines increasingly depend on model judges, and weak judge reliability can quietly distort research conclusions and product tuning.

### 4. Think in Latent Thoughts: A New Paradigm for Gloss-Free Sign Language Translation
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15301v1
- Summary: The paper argues that sign language translation should be treated as a cross-modal reasoning problem rather than a direct video-to-text mapping task. It introduces a reasoning-driven framework with latent intermediate thoughts between visual input and generated language. The idea is compelling because it reframes translation around compositional understanding, which may be necessary for richer multimodal communication tasks beyond sign language itself.

### 5. AnimationBench: Are Video Models Good at Character-Centric Animation?
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15299v1
- Summary: AnimationBench targets a gap in video-model evaluation by focusing on stylized, character-centric animation rather than only realistic video generation. The benchmark emphasizes issues such as character consistency, exaggerated motion, and flexible prompt handling. This is timely because video generation progress is broadening into more commercial and creative settings where animation-specific quality criteria matter.

### 6. How Do LLMs and VLMs Understand Viewpoint Rotation Without Vision? An Interpretability Study
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15294v1
- Summary: This paper examines whether language and vision-language models can perform viewpoint-rotation reasoning even when explicit visual input is absent. Rather than only reporting task accuracy, it focuses on interpretability to understand the mechanisms behind text-only spatial reasoning. The work is interesting because it probes how far linguistic structure alone can carry spatial intelligence, which is important for reasoning benchmarks and agent planning alike.

### 7. AD4AD: Benchmarking Visual Anomaly Detection Models for Safer Autonomous Driving
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15291v1
- Summary: AD4AD benchmarks visual anomaly detection for autonomous driving, where performance can degrade sharply once the system encounters unusual conditions. The paper is explicitly motivated by safety, since perception failures in driving have direct physical consequences. This makes it valuable as a deployment-oriented benchmark that stresses robustness under distribution shift instead of only nominal accuracy.

### 8. RAD-2: Scaling Reinforcement Learning in a Generator-Discriminator Framework
- Date: 2026-04-16
- URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15308v1
- Summary: RAD-2 targets closed-loop autonomous driving by combining a generator-discriminator framework with reinforcement learning to better handle multimodal futures and interactive planning. The motivation is that imitation-only training often leaves diffusion-based planners unstable and under-corrected in realistic rollouts. It matters because autonomous systems increasingly need training recipes that stay robust when the model’s own decisions shape the next state.

## Themes
- Evaluation work is becoming more realistic, granular, and reliability-aware.
- Multimodal agents are being tested on end-to-end tasks instead of isolated components.
- Researchers are probing reasoning structure rather than relying only on leaderboard outcomes.
- Safety-critical perception and closed-loop decision making remain central deployment challenges.
