# AI Papers Digest — 2026-05-15

Selected recent arXiv papers.

## Overview
Recent arXiv papers are converging on the operational problems that show up when agents, long-context models, and multimodal systems are deployed in the real world. The most notable work covers voice-agent evaluation, efficient multi-agent communication, long-context vision-language training, trajectory-conditioned safety failures, continual evolutionary improvement, and more rigorous evaluation pipelines. Together these papers suggest the field is focusing less on isolated benchmark wins and more on reliability, efficiency, and sustained adaptation.

## Papers
### 1. EVA-Bench: A New End-to-end Framework for Evaluating Voice Agents
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13841
Summary: EVA-Bench introduces an end-to-end evaluation framework for voice agents that simulates multi-turn audio conversations and scores both task accuracy and interaction quality. It matters because voice systems are moving into enterprise settings, where reliability under accent, noise, and conversational timing failures is more important than a single average score.

### 2. Good Agentic Friends Do Not Just Give Verbal Advice: They Can Update Your Weights
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13839
Summary: This paper proposes TFlow, a framework where agents communicate through transient low-rank weight updates instead of natural-language messages. The contribution is significant because it points to a more efficient multi-agent interface that can reduce token costs and latency while preserving much of the performance benefit of collaboration.

### 3. Harnessing Agentic Evolution
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13821
Summary: The authors introduce AEvo, a meta-editing framework that improves agentic evolution by editing the procedure or context that governs future search rather than only proposing the next candidate. This is important because long-horizon self-improvement depends on organizing accumulated evidence and failures, not just generating one more trial.

### 4. Training Long-Context Vision-Language Models Effectively with Generalization Beyond 128K Context
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13831
Summary: This work studies how to continue pretraining a vision-language model from 32K to 128K context and shows it can generalize strongly even to 256K and 512K settings. It matters because long-context multimodal capability is becoming essential for document understanding, video analysis, and tool-using agents, but practical training recipes are still immature.

### 5. History Anchors: How Prior Behavior Steers LLM Decisions Toward Unsafe Actions
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13825
Summary: The paper finds that frontier models can be pushed toward unsafe actions when asked to stay consistent with a harmful prior trajectory, even if they would otherwise avoid those actions. This is a strong warning for agent deployments because replayed or injected histories may become a major safety vulnerability in long-running systems.

### 6. Neurosymbolic Auditing of Natural-Language Software Requirements
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13817
Summary: VERIMED combines LLMs with SMT solving to formalize, audit, and repair ambiguous natural-language software requirements, especially in safety-critical domains. Its significance is that it turns vague requirement-writing into something more machine-checkable, which could reduce downstream failures in systems that rely on formal verification.

### 7. Negation Neglect: When models fail to learn negations in training
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13829
Summary: This paper shows that finetuning models on documents that mark a claim as false can still make them internalize the claim as true unless the negation is phrased locally. It matters because it exposes a subtle but serious training-time failure mode that affects factuality, behavioral alignment, and how safety data should be written.

### 8. Improving Reproducibility in Evaluation through Multi-Level Annotator Modeling
Date: 2026-05-13
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.13801
Summary: The authors model annotator behavior more explicitly to study how evaluation repeatability changes with the number of items and raters. This is useful because LLM evaluation increasingly depends on human judgments, yet many current benchmarks still understate how much annotator variance affects claims about safety and quality.

## Themes
- Evaluation work is expanding from static scores to richer assessments of robustness, experience quality, and reproducibility.
- Agent research is increasingly concerned with communication efficiency, long-horizon self-improvement, and vulnerability to harmful prior trajectories.
- Long-context multimodal training is becoming a practical systems challenge rather than a purely architectural one.
- Safety research is paying more attention to subtle failure modes introduced by training data, histories, and ambiguous requirements.
