# AI Papers Digest — 2026-05-07
Selected recent arXiv papers.

## Overview
The freshest arXiv papers emphasize the same shift visible in product news: AI progress now depends on better system design around models, not just bigger base capabilities. Recent work focuses on stronger search agents, more faithful multimodal generation, richer safety evaluation, longitudinal alignment measurement, and auditable multi-agent decision support. Together, these papers suggest the field is pushing toward AI systems that are cheaper to trust, easier to evaluate, and more useful in complex real-world settings.

## Papers
### 1. OpenSeeker-v2: Pushing the Limits of Search Agents with Informative and High-Difficulty Trajectories
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04036
Summary: This paper argues that a relatively simple supervised fine-tuning pipeline can produce a frontier search agent when the training data is sufficiently informative and difficult. That matters because it challenges the idea that top-tier agent performance requires an expensive full stack of continual pretraining and reinforcement learning. If the result holds up, it lowers the barrier for academic teams to build competitive agentic search systems.

### 2. Large Language Models are Universal Reasoners for Visual Generation
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04040
Summary: The authors propose UniReasoner, which uses an LLM to critique a coarse visual draft and feed explicit corrective guidance back into image generation. This matters because it turns a model’s understanding strength into a direct mechanism for improving compositional faithfulness, one of the hardest problems in text-to-image systems. It also points toward a broader pattern in which reasoning modules supervise generative modules inside multimodal pipelines.

### 3. Safety and accuracy follow different scaling laws in clinical large language models
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04039
Summary: This work shows that stronger average benchmark accuracy in clinical LLMs does not automatically translate into safer behavior, especially when retrieval design and evidence quality are weak. That matters because high-stakes domains care more about rare dangerous failures than about mean score improvements. The paper gives a concrete framework for treating safety as a deployment property that must be engineered and measured separately from capability.

### 4. Audio-Visual Intelligence in Large Foundation Models
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04045
Summary: This survey organizes the rapidly growing field of audio-visual intelligence across understanding, generation, and interaction tasks in large foundation models. That matters because progress in unified audio-video systems is currently fragmented across inconsistent task definitions and evaluation practices. A strong taxonomy and benchmark map can make it easier to compare approaches and identify the real open problems in controllability, synchronization, and safety.

### 5. Rethinking Reasoning-Intensive Retrieval: Evaluating and Advancing Retrievers in Agentic Search Systems
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04018
Summary: The paper introduces BRIGHT-Pro and synthetic training data designed to evaluate and improve retrieval for agentic search systems that need complementary evidence rather than simple topical matches. This matters because many search agents fail not at reasoning itself but at assembling the right evidence portfolio across iterative steps. Better retrieval evaluation could materially improve the reliability of deep research and search-agent products.

### 6. Redefining AI Red Teaming in the Agentic Era: From Weeks to Hours
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04019
Summary: The authors present an agentic red-teaming interface that automates workflow construction across attacks, transforms, and scorers, reducing the manual effort required to probe model vulnerabilities. This matters because security evaluation is becoming too workflow-heavy to scale by hand as systems turn multimodal and multi-agent. An automated red-teaming layer could make adversarial testing faster, broader, and more routine in production pipelines.

### 7. Stayin' Aligned Over Time: Towards Longitudinal Human-LLM Alignment via Contextual Reflection and Privacy-Preserving Behavioral Data
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04029
Summary: This paper argues that one-shot preference capture misses how users reassess model outputs after real-world consequences unfold, and proposes a framework for longitudinal alignment measurement. This matters because alignment data often treats preference as static when many consequential AI interactions are not. The approach could produce richer supervision for systems that must stay helpful over time rather than only look good at the moment of response.

### 8. Physics-Grounded Multi-Agent Architecture for Traceable, Risk-Aware Human-AI Decision Support in Manufacturing
Date: 2026-05-05
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.04003
Summary: The paper presents MAKA, a human-in-the-loop multi-agent architecture that separates routing, quantitative tools, retrieval, and critic-based verification for high-stakes manufacturing decisions. This matters because industrial deployments need auditable provenance and physically grounded checks, not just fluent recommendations. It is a useful example of how multi-agent systems can be structured to make AI assistance traceable and risk-aware in real operational settings.

## Themes
- Search and retrieval are becoming central bottlenecks for high-performing agent systems.
- Multimodal generation research is increasingly using reasoning modules to improve faithfulness and control.
- Safety evaluation is maturing into a first-class systems problem rather than a side effect of model scaling.
- Alignment work is expanding beyond immediate preferences toward longitudinal, real-world feedback loops.
- High-stakes deployments are pushing multi-agent architectures toward auditability, grounded tools, and explicit verification.
