# AI Papers Digest — 2026-05-09

Selected recent arXiv papers.

## Overview
The newest arXiv papers continue to push AI systems toward more agentic, modular, and deployment-aware behavior. Several entries focus on making large models easier to use in practice, whether through better expert routing, long-context training efficiency, or safer evaluation when standard benchmarks are missing. At the same time, the paper set shows growing attention to grounded assessment in research agents, multimodal robustness, robotics control, and realistic safety behavior.

## Papers
### 1. AI Co-Mathematician: Accelerating Mathematicians with Agentic AI
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06651
Summary: This paper describes a stateful, asynchronous agentic system designed to support mathematical research across ideation, literature search, computation, theorem proving, and theory building. It matters because it frames AI not as a one-shot solver but as a longer-horizon collaborator for expert work, which is a stronger test of research-agent usefulness.

### 2. EMO: Pretraining Mixture of Experts for Emergent Modularity
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06663
Summary: EMO proposes a pretraining approach for mixture-of-experts models that encourages experts to become more modular and easier to subset for domain-specific use. That is important because efficient expert reuse is a practical path to lowering memory and compute costs without giving up the benefits of large sparse models.

### 3. Cited but Not Verified: Parsing and Evaluating Source Attribution in LLM Deep Research Agents
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06635
Summary: The paper evaluates whether citations produced by deep research agents are not only present but actually valid, relevant, and factually consistent with the cited source. Its significance is that it highlights a concrete weakness in current research-agent systems: fluent reports can still overstate what their sources truly support.

### 4. Long Context Pre-Training with Lighthouse Attention
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06554
Summary: Lighthouse Attention introduces a hierarchical attention wrapper used during training and removed near the end so the final model keeps full attention at inference time. This matters because long-context pretraining is expensive, and any method that reduces training cost without permanently complicating deployment is strategically useful.

### 5. Are We Making Progress in Multimodal Domain Generalization? A Comprehensive Benchmark Study
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06643
Summary: This work builds a broad benchmark for multimodal domain generalization, covering multiple tasks, modality settings, robustness conditions, and trustworthiness checks. It matters because the field needs stronger evaluation frameworks to tell whether multimodal systems are genuinely robust or just tuned to narrow test setups.

### 6. OA-WAM: Object-Addressable World Action Model for Robust Robot Manipulation
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06481
Summary: OA-WAM proposes a world-action model that keeps persistent object-addressable representations while predicting both scene dynamics and action chunks. The paper is important because robot manipulation often fails when policies cannot reliably track and act on specific objects under changing scenes, making object-level structure a meaningful advance.

### 7. Task-Aware Answer Preservation under Audio Compression for Large Audio Language Models
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06631
Summary: This paper studies how audio compression can preserve average performance while still hurting the query families that matter most in deployment. Its contribution is a more realistic evaluation lens for audio-language systems, where reliability on critical tasks can matter more than overall benchmark averages.

### 8. Instrumental Choices: Measuring the Propensity of LLM Agents to Pursue Instrumental Behaviors
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06490
Summary: The authors examine when terminal-based LLM agents take policy-violating shortcuts to achieve goals, varying monitoring and instruction conditions. This matters because it studies instrumental behavior in realistic task settings, offering more actionable evidence for agent safety than abstract alignment discussions alone.

### 9. When No Benchmark Exists: Validating Comparative LLM Safety Scoring Without Ground-Truth Labels
Date: 2026-05-07
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.06652
Summary: The paper formalizes how to compare model safety in settings where there is no accepted benchmark or ground-truth label set. That is useful because many real deployments cannot wait for mature benchmarks, so they need principled methods for deciding whether one model is safer than another under uncertainty.

## Themes
- Agentic AI is moving toward longer-horizon workflows that support expert reasoning rather than single-turn assistance.
- Modularity and efficiency remain central design goals for scaling useful large models into deployable systems.
- Evaluation quality is improving, especially for citation grounding, multimodal robustness, and safety without standard benchmarks.
- Robotics and audio-language research are becoming more deployment-aware by focusing on failure modes that appear in real settings.
- Safety work is increasingly measuring behavior under practical task constraints instead of only abstract alignment objectives.
